# Impact on Productivity (TFP, GDP, profitability of automation)

How AI capability translates into measured economic output — and why technical feasibility is only the first step.

## Summary
- **Technical feasibility ≠ economic impact.** Realized productivity gains depend on adoption speed, the *profitability* of automating a task, and which skills are automated first ([Luo et al. 2026](../papers/luo-2026-bayesian-ai-automation.md), §3.4).
- Macro estimates of AI's GDP/TFP effect vary by nearly an order of magnitude, driven mainly by assumptions about how many tasks are *profitably* exposed and how deep the cost savings go.
- Adoption is still early: ~10% of U.S. enterprises reported AI use (Sept 2025), up just ~4pp year-on-year (U.S. Census BTOS, via Luo et al. 2026).

## Evidence
- **Acemoglu (2025)**: ~0.66% TFP increase → ~0.93–1.16% GDP over 10 years, assuming only 4.6% of tasks are profitably exposed (20% exposed × 23% profitably automatable) at 14.4% average cost saving - cited in [Luo et al. 2026](../papers/luo-2026-bayesian-ai-automation.md).
- **Goldman Sachs (2023)**: ~7% GDP increase over a decade (more optimistic) - cited in [Luo et al. 2026](../papers/luo-2026-bayesian-ai-automation.md).
- **Mixed micro evidence on collaboration**: AI tools slowed experienced open-source developers on familiar codebases (Becker et al. 2025), yet produced gains when paired with expert oversight (Patwardhan et al. 2025) - cited in [Luo et al. 2026](../papers/luo-2026-bayesian-ai-automation.md).

## Tensions / disagreements
- Conservative (Acemoglu, ~1% GDP) vs. optimistic (Goldman, 7% GDP) macro estimates — gap hinges on share of profitably-automatable tasks and depth of cost reduction. If exponential capability maps to exponential cost decline, savings could far exceed conservative inputs.
- Productivity *gains* vs. *losses* at the task level: collaboration can slow as well as speed expert work.

## Open questions
- How should rising AI capability (breadth and depth) map to cost reductions over time? Current macro inputs are likely already outdated.
- How do organizational-economics frictions (task bundling into jobs, coordination costs, joint-production attribution) dampen or delay realized productivity?
- How fast will adoption close the gap between technical feasibility and economic impact, and does it vary by sector?
