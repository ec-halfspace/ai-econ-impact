# Automation and New Tasks: How Technology Displaces and Reinstates Labor

- **Authors / Year**: Daron Acemoglu, Pascual Restrepo / 2019
- **Venue / Source**: Journal of Economic Perspectives 33(2), 3-30
- **PDF**: ../../papers/acemoglu-restrepo-2019-automation-and-new-tasks-how-technology-displaces-and-reinstates-labor.pdf
- **Themes**: [[task-to-role-aggregation]], [[jobs]], [[productivity]], [[wages]]

## Research question(s)
- How does technology reshape labour demand once production is modelled as **tasks allocated to capital or labour**, and the *task content of production* can shift?

## Methodology
- Canonical **task-based framework** (building on Zeira 1998; Acemoglu-Autor 2011; Acemoglu-Restrepo 2018a/b). Output requires a range of tasks; each is allocated to capital or labour by comparative advantage/cost.
- Decomposes technology's effect into: **displacement effect** (capital takes over labour tasks -> lowers labour share/demand), **productivity effect** (cheaper task completion raises output and hence labour demand in non-automated tasks), and the **reinstatement effect** (new labour-intensive tasks shift task content back toward labour).

## Main results
- Net labour-demand effect of automation is **ambiguous**: displacement vs. productivity + reinstatement.
- Automation alone => shrinking labour share and task set; historically counterbalanced by **new tasks** (reinstatement). ~half of 1980-2015 US employment growth was in occupations whose titles/tasks changed.
- "So-so automation" (much displacement, little productivity gain) is the worst case for labour.

## Relevance to AI economic impact
- **The theoretical backbone** for the whole tasks -> jobs -> economy programme and for workstream A. Supplies: (i) the task-allocation/comparative-advantage logic A's aggregator formalises at occupation scale; (ii) **reinstatement / new tasks** = thread E and a caution that a *static* task list understates the future; (iii) the displacement-vs-productivity tension A must represent.

## Limitations & open questions
- Framework/perspective piece, not an empirical occupation-level aggregator; no AI-specific forecast inputs.
- Reinstatement is central but hard to forecast (new tasks are, by definition, not yet in O*NET).
