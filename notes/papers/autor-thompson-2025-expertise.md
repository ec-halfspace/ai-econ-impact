# Expertise

- **Authors / Year**: David Autor, Neil Thompson / 2025 (NBER WP 33941, June 2025; Schumpeter Lecture)
- **Venue / Source**: NBER Working Paper (not peer-reviewed)
- **PDF**: ../../papers/Autor_Thomspon_expertise_2025.pdf
- **Themes**: [[task-to-role-aggregation]], [[jobs]], [[wages]]

## Research question(s)
- When job tasks are automated, does this **augment or diminish** the value of labour in the tasks that remain?
- Why do occupations with *similar* automation exposure experience *opposite* wage/employment outcomes?

## Methodology
- Conceptual **"expertise" model of occupational task bundling**, built on two pillars absent from exposure/task models: **expertise** (hierarchical, task-specific human capital that gates entry) and **indivisibility** (all tasks bundled in an occupation must be done by each worker in it).
- Prediction: automation's effect depends on whether the **removed tasks were expert or inexpert** relative to the remaining bundle. Removing *inexpert* tasks raises the occupation's expertise requirement -> higher wages, fewer qualified workers; removing *expert* tasks lowers it -> lower wages, easier entry (more employment).
- Novel **content-agnostic method to measure task expertise**; applied to four decades of task change (task removal + addition) across occupations.

## Data
- O*NET tasks + long-run US occupational wage/employment (four decades).

## Main results
- Automation **raised wages / cut employment** in occupations where it eliminated *inexpert* tasks; **lowered wages / raised employment** where it eliminated *expert* tasks.
- These effects are **distinct from (and for employment, opposite to)** the effects of changing task *quantities* (the standard displacement channel).
- Resolves the puzzle of why routine-task automation lowered employment but often *raised* wages in routine-intensive occupations.

## Relevance to AI economic impact
- **For workstream A: a refinement of gap #2, not a competitor.** It formalises *which* remaining tasks matter after automation (the expertise of the residual) and the resulting **wage/skill** direction - a layer A can add on top of the bottleneck/threshold automation-timing model. Its "indivisibility" (task bundling) is complementary to the O-ring bottleneck idea.
- Is a **conceptual + retrospective** model of expertise reallocation and wages; it is **not** a forecast-ready task->role automation aggregator producing occupation automation scores/timelines. So it informs A's wage/skill implications, but does not pre-empt the aggregation contribution.

## Limitations & open questions
- Stylised unidimensional expertise ranking (tractability assumption).
- Retrospective/conceptual; not a forward capability-forecast aggregator.
- Doesn't price feasibility/profitability or model the automation *timing* directly.
